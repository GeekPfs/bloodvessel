safety & themes
this game is a discussion of colonialism and state
violence, the objects created for it and the lives they
have. it contains death, injuries, and unhappy endings.
in this game you play as a spaceship that was created to be
a tool to colonial violence. the reconciliation that you
are offered in this game is not a reconciliation for
colonialism — it is an exploration of what good can come
from objects that were created for evil.
i would like to acknowledge that i am white, and my writing
about colonialism is influenced by that whiteness. i have
included some recommendations of games about colonialism
written from colonized and marginalized perspectives at the
end of this book.
finally, blood vessels is a lonely game. please take breaks
whenever you need — i have included reminders to take
breaks in the text.
overview
you were created for war.
you were built somewhere terrible and austere. your
pieces came from places the galaxy wide, planets that
your creators, the colonizers, clutch like strangling
vines.
you must make your own path now.
blood vessels is a roleplaying game about a single
spaceship. it was designed to be a solo-rpg experience,
but you are welcome to add as many players as you want.
you will need 40 minutes to an hour, and a few pieces of
paper to write on.
this game is a conversation between you and me. you
decide who “you” is by making decisions about your
character and world throughout the game, and writing
them down. some things you write will change throughout
the game, at your choice. you decide who “me” is, too.
“me” can be someone you’re narrating to, or your own
memory, or someone else.
the conversation utilizes the meat of this book, a
series of prompts that you can use as you wish. you
might play this game by jotting down some notes and
sketches, or you might play it by stringing thoughts
together into journal entries. you can play it alone or
with partners; in one sitting or across several days;
while you work or while you sleep. the only thing i
suggest strongly is that you follow the prompts in
order.

game concepts
a playbook is a collection of starting questions and
options you use to build your spaceship in the
beginning. there are four in this book: the carrier, the
cruiser, the fighter, and the skull. during this phase,
you can add as much detail to your character as you
like. if it helps, you might draw it — a simple floor
plan and/or a diagram of the outside should do the
trick.
a trait is a short descriptor of something that is
meaningful to your ship right now. traits can be
specific or broad, physical or metaphorical. they will
change many times throughout the game. example lists of
traits can be found on each playbook. lore and details
are never limited to traits (which is to say: your
character and world are much more than bulleted lists),
but if a detail feels important enough to put on a list,
go ahead and make it a trait.
a key question is a question with a dynamic answer which
you will be asked to refer to and perhaps change
throughout the game. there are four in each playbook,
and you choose one or more to answer. give space next to
these for the answers to be changed or added to.
an act is a unit of story, during which a small arc of
events happen. there are three acts in this game. before
you play the first act, you will choose a playbook. after
you play the third act, you can play the epilogue.
when you’re ready, choose a playbook and begin.

the carrier
[a playbook]
Choose 1+ directive:
- To carry an uncountable number of weapons, each
capable of destroying a person
- To carry a weapon capable of destroying a fleet of
ships
- To carry a weapon capable of destroying an entire
planet
- To carry a weapon capable of destroying yourself
Choose 2+ traits, plus 1+ of your own creation:
+ metallic
+ sleek
+ black
+ chrome
+ blinding
+ piercing
+ subtle
+ silent
+ intimidating
+ blocky
+ organic
+ patchwork
+ many rooms
+ a central hangar
+ many small hangars
+ secrets tucked away
+ a control room with only enough
space for one person
+ a discreet appearance
+ a weapon visible on the outside of
the ship
Choose and answer 1+ key question:
- What is your name?
- What planet is your home? Describe the place on it
where you rest.
- What secrets or treasures of the empire do you hold
inside?
- Do you feel? Do you think? If so, how do you think
of yourself?
Then:
- What is your shame? What act of violence did you
commit or enable? Who used you for power, and how?
You will never be able to change this.
Finally:
- How were you liberated? Did you falter and crash?
Did they lose track of you somewhere hidden? Were
you hijacked? Did you escape on your own somehow?
When you’re ready, turn to act 1.

act 1 of 3
[the gash]
act 1 takes place directly after you are liberated.
Tell me:
- Where do you come to rest after your liberation?
- How long do you rest before you are found and
seized?
- What do you spend this time wondering?
Mix and match from each column to create 1+ person
who finds you:
+ a mechanic
+ an herbalist
+ a rascal
+ a loving soul
+ someone else
+ from this planet
+ from a dune planet
+ from a busy planet
+ from a quiet planet
+ from somewhere else
Then, for each, decide what they want from you, and
tell me a little more about them:
- they want to grow something beautiful in your
largest room
- they want to find safety in your smallest room
- they want to use you to turn their life around
- they want to have a family inside you
- they want to do something else with you
Now choose and answer 1+:
- What parts of you startle your new crew?
- What parts of you are they confident they can use?
- What parts of you are left untouched or unnoticed
by this crew?
- While exploring you, someone stumbles upon the
evidence of a horrific atrocity. How do they react?
When you’re ready, proceed to the next page.

15
epilogue
[the long rest]
Complete as much of this section as you must, then stop,
put this game down, and take a long break.
You dream.
- The people who came with you along the way —
they’re not gone, not really. Who appears to you in
a dream? What do they tell you?
You observe.
A ship passes overhead. Describe it, then choose 1 or
both:
- Tell me what you have in common with this ship.
- Tell me how you’re nothing like this ship.
You fade.
- What does time take away from you? Change something
about your appearance to mark the decline of your
physical state.
You dream.
- Who or what appears to you in a dream? How do they
try to comfort you? Does it work?
You observe.
What do you see on this world? Does it trouble you? Does
it offer you comfort? Choose 1 or both:
- Tell me how you’ve become like the nature of this
world.
- Tell me how you’re nothing like the nature of this
world.
You fade.
- What does time take away from you? What does time
give to you? Change something about your appearance
to mark the decline of your physical state.
Repeat as much of this section as you must.
Then, when you’re ready, proceed to the next page.

This game was created on the unceded ancestral land of
the Duwamish People past and present. I would like to
honor with gratitude the land itself and the Duwamish
Tribe.
Blood Vessels is a hack of Jack Harrison’s Bucket of
Bolts. It was written in September 2021 as a
submission to the Lost & Found Jam. Other inspirations
include the dozens of hours I’ve spent contemplating
the war crimes I have committed in Twilight Imperium.

I like that address colonialism from colonized
perspectives. Please check them out!
